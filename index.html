Before You Start - Daily Checks

Check These Every Time:

  • Brakes work properly
  • Steering moves smoothly
  • Forks are not bent or cracked
  • Warning lights and horn work
  • No oil or water leaks
  • Tyres are in good condition

üö¶ Click to reveal: The Golden Rules of Forklift Safety

  1. Speed: Never go faster than walking pace
  2. Loads: Keep forks low when moving
  3. Corners: Slow down and sound your horn
  4. People: Never let anyone ride on the forklift
  5. Parking: Lower forks, apply handbrake, remove key

Working Around Forklifts

üë• If You're NOT the Driver

  • Stay away from operating forklifts
  • Never walk under raised loads
  • Make eye contact with drivers before crossing their path
  • Use designated walkways

üö® Real Example

A worker fell 8 metres from forklift forks when trying to change a light bulb. The company was fined ¬£200,000. Never stand on forklift forks or pallets for any reason!

üÜò What to do in an emergency?

  • Stop the forklift immediately
  • Apply the handbrake
  • Turn off the engine
  • Get help from a supervisor
  • Report the incident

üì¶ Pallet Trucks - Manual and Electric

‚ñº

üí° Quick Fact

Pallet trucks cause many back injuries. Most happen because people don't follow simple safety rules.

Manual Pallet Trucks

‚úÖ Safe Use Steps

  1. Check: Wheels roll freely, handle works
  2. Position: Forks fully under the pallet
  3. Lift: Pump handle until load clears floor
  4. Move: Keep load low, push don't pull
  5. Watch: Look where you're going

‚öñÔ∏è Weight Limits Matter

  • Manual trucks: Usually 2-3 tonnes maximum
  • Check the label on your truck
  • Never exceed the limit
  • Get help with heavy loads

Electric Pallet Trucks

Before Each Use:

  • Battery charge level is good
  • Emergency stop button works
  • Forks move up and down smoothly
  • Steering handle is secure
  • Warning devices work

‚öôÔ∏è Machine Safety & Guarding

‚ñº

‚ö†Ô∏è Moving Parts are Dangerous!

Machines can cause serious injuries like cuts, crushing, or even amputation if you're not careful. Safety guards are there to protect you.

NEVER

Tamper with, bypass, or remove a safety guard or interlock while a machine is operating.

What are Guards For?

Guards are physical barriers that prevent you from coming into contact with dangerous moving parts, hot surfaces, or electrical hazards.

ü§î Click to learn: What is a Safety Interlock?

An interlock is a special safety switch connected to a guard (like a gate or door).

  • If you open the guard, the interlock automatically stops the machine.
  • The machine cannot restart until the guard is closed again.
  • Think of it like your microwave: it stops the second you open the door. Never try to defeat these switches!

Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) for Cleaning & Maintenance

üõë Before You Clean or Fix a Machine

If you need to work on a machine, it must be made safe first using a Lockout/Tagout procedure. This means:

  1. The machine is turned off.
  2. The power source (e.g., electrical switch) is locked in the 'OFF' position with a padlock.
  3. A tag is attached saying "DANGER - DO NOT OPERATE".

Only trained and authorised staff can perform LOTO.

Your Responsibilities:

  • Never use a machine if the guard is missing or damaged.
  • Report any faulty guards or interlocks to your supervisor immediately.
  • Do not wear loose clothing, jewellery, or long, untied hair around machinery.
  • Follow all LOTO procedures you have been trained on.
  • Never try to "fix" a jam while the machine is running.

üß™ Chemical Safety - COSHH Regulations

‚ñº

‚ò¢Ô∏è Important

Food factories use many cleaning chemicals. Under UK COSHH regulations, you must know how to use them safely.

Understanding Chemical Labels

üè∑Ô∏è What to Look For

  • Product name: What the chemical is called
  • Hazard symbols: Pictures showing dangers
  • Signal words: "Danger" or "Warning"
  • Safety instructions: How to use safely
  • First aid info: What to do if something goes wrong

üßº Click to see: Common Cleaning Chemicals in Food Factories

Alkaline Cleaners

Remove grease and proteins

Danger: Burns skin

Acid Cleaners

Remove mineral deposits

Danger: Burns skin and eyes

Sanitisers

Kill bacteria and germs

Danger: Breathing problems

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Always Wear:

  • Chemical-resistant gloves
  • Safety goggles or face shield
  • Apron or protective clothing
  • Non-slip safety boots
  • Respirator if required

üöø Spill Procedures

  1. Stop: Don't panic
  2. Contain: Stop the spill spreading
  3. Alert: Tell others in the area
  4. Clean: Follow the data sheet instructions
  5. Report: Tell your supervisor

ü©π Emergency First Aid for Chemical Contact

If chemicals get on skin:

  • Remove contaminated clothing
  • Rinse with water for at least 20 minutes
  • Get medical help

If chemicals get in eyes:

  • Rinse with clean water for at least 20 minutes
  • Hold eyelids open
  • Get medical help immediately

If chemicals are swallowed:

  • DO NOT make the person sick
  • Rinse mouth with water
  • Get medical help immediately

ü™ú Ladder Safety - Working at Height

‚ñº

üìä UK Statistics

Falls from height cause about 40 deaths and thousands of injuries each year in UK workplaces. Many involve ladders.

üé¨ Watch: FLT Safety

This short video gives some ladder safety pointers.

Before You Climb - Ladder Inspection

Check Every Time:

  • Rungs are not bent, cracked or missing
  • Side rails are straight and undamaged
  • Feet have good grip pads
  • Locks and hinges work properly
  • No oil, grease or other slippery substances
  • Labels are clear and readable

üìê The 4-to-1 Rule - Click to Learn

How to position your ladder safely:

For every 4 units of height, the bottom should be 1 unit away from the wall.

Example: If the ladder reaches 4 metres high, place the bottom 1 metre from the wall.

  • Too steep = ladder falls backwards
  • Too shallow = ladder slides out
  • Just right = safe and stable

The Three-Point Contact Rule

ü§ù Always Keep Three Points of Contact

This means either:

  • Two hands and one foot on the ladder, OR
  • Two feet and one hand on the ladder

Never: Carry tools in your hands while climbing. Use a tool belt or rope.

üö´ When NOT to Use Ladders

  • In high winds or bad weather
  • When you feel dizzy or unwell
  • For tasks lasting more than 30 minutes
  • When you need both hands to work
  • Near electrical equipment when wet

‚ñº

üìà The Facts

Manual handling injuries account for around one-third of all workplace injuries in UK food factories. Most affect the back.

üé¨ Watch: Safe Lifting Technique

Safe Lifting Technique

The LITE Method:

  • Look at the load - check weight and contents
  • Individual capability - can you manage it?
  • Task requirements - how far to carry?
  • Environment - is the route clear?

üìã Click for: Step-by-Step Safe Lifting

  1. Plan your lift: Where will you put the load?
  2. Position yourself: Feet apart, one slightly forward
  3. Get a good grip: Use both hands, fingers underneath
  4. Bend your knees: Keep your back straight
  5. Lift smoothly: No sudden movements
  6. Keep load close: Hold it against your body
  7. Move your feet: Don't twist your back
  8. Put down carefully: Same technique in reverse

‚öñÔ∏è Weight Guidelines

Maximum safe lifting weights (healthy adults):

  • Men: 25kg from waist height
  • Women: 16kg from waist height
  • Overhead lifting: Much less - get help!
  • If you're unsure: Ask for help or use equipment

Team Lifting

üë• When Two People Lift Together

  • Choose people of similar height
  • One person gives the commands
  • Practice the lift first
  • Move at the pace of the slower person
  • Communicate throughout
